A 54-year-old woman is evaluated for 4 months of fatigue, mild weight gain, and constipation. She attributes her symptoms to a new desk job. Examination shows a heart rate of 58/min and slightly coarse, dry skin; the relaxation phase of her ankle reflexes is prolonged. A lipid panel obtained for cardiovascular screening shows an LDL cholesterol of 188 mg/dL, up from 122 mg/dL two years ago. She takes no medications. Serum creatine kinase is mildly elevated.
❓ Which of the following laboratory patterns would best explain both her symptoms and the change in her lipid panel?
A
Elevated TSH with elevated free T4
B
Elevated TSH with low free T4
C
Normal TSH with low total T4 and normal free T4
D
Suppressed TSH with elevated free T4
E
Low TSH with low free T4
A
High TSH with high free T4 suggests a TSH-secreting adenoma or assay interference—rare and not fitting this hypothyroid clinical picture.
B
Correct. Hypothyroidism reduces LDL-receptor expression and lipoprotein clearance, so new or worsening hypercholesterolemia (and a mildly elevated CK from myopathy) is a classic secondary cause—here unifying her fatigue, bradycardia, delayed reflex relaxation, and rising LDL. Primary hypothyroidism is defined biochemically by a HIGH TSH with a LOW free T4. Treating with levothyroxine typically improves the lipid profile.
C
A low total T4 with normal free T4 and normal TSH reflects a thyroxine-binding globulin change (e.g., estrogen), not true hypothyroidism, and would not cause these symptoms or LDL rise.
D
Suppressed TSH with high free T4 is hyperthyroidism, which lowers (not raises) cholesterol and causes weight loss and tachycardia—opposite to this patient.
E
Low TSH with low free T4 indicates central hypothyroidism; while it could cause hypothyroid symptoms, it is far less common and is not the best single explanation without pituitary clues.
Confirming, treating, and special situations
Primary hypothyroidism is confirmed by a high TSH with low free T4; the most common cause in iodine-sufficient areas is Hashimoto thyroiditis (positive anti-TPO antibodies). Treat with levothyroxine and recheck TSH every 6–8 weeks, titrating to a goal TSH of roughly 0.5–5.0. Two high-yield wrinkles: in pregnancy, increased thyroxine-binding globulin raises levothyroxine requirements—increase the dose once pregnancy is confirmed; and subclinical hypothyroidism (high TSH, normal T4) is generally treated when TSH is >10 or the patient is symptomatic/pregnant.
TSH is the single best screening test: a HIGH TSH localizes the problem to the gland itself (primary), while a LOW/normal TSH with low T4 points to the pituitary (central).
USMLE Pearl: Unexplained or worsening hypercholesterolemia (± elevated CK) is a classic clue to hypothyroidism—check a TSH. Primary hypothyroidism = HIGH TSH + LOW free T4; treating it improves the lipid panel. Always consider secondary causes before escalating statin therapy.
A 24-year-old woman has 3 months of palpitations, a 5-kg unintentional weight loss, and irritability. Heart rate is 112/min and irregular at times; she has a fine tremor and warm, moist skin, and a symmetrically enlarged thyroid without tenderness. TSH is undetectable and free T4 is elevated. A radioactive iodine uptake scan shows diffusely INCREASED uptake. Her eyes show mild proptosis on exophthalmometry. This is the first of two linked questions about the same patient.
❓ Which of the following best explains the increased radioiodine uptake in this patient?
A
Autonomous hormone production from a single nodule
B
Ingestion of exogenous thyroid hormone
C
Leakage of preformed hormone from inflamed follicles
D
Iodine-induced hormone synthesis from a recent contrast load
E
Autoantibodies that stimulate the TSH receptor
A
A single autonomous nodule would show focal increased uptake with suppression of the rest of the gland, not diffuse uptake, and would not cause orbitopathy.
B
Exogenous hormone suppresses TSH and the gland, giving LOW uptake (and low thyroglobulin).
C
Leakage from inflamed follicles (thyroiditis) releases stored hormone WITHOUT new synthesis, so uptake is LOW, not increased.
D
Iodine-induced thyrotoxicosis (Jod-Basedow) typically shows low uptake because the gland is already iodine-loaded.
E
Correct. Diffusely INCREASED uptake means the gland is actively trapping iodine and synthesizing hormone under stimulation—in a young woman with symmetric goiter and orbitopathy, this is Graves disease, caused by TSH-receptor–stimulating antibodies. The orbitopathy is essentially specific to Graves among the thyrotoxic causes.
Definitive options and their trade-offs
After beta-blockade controls symptoms, definitive treatment for Graves is one of three: a thionamide (methimazole, or PTU in the first trimester of pregnancy), radioactive iodine ablation (avoid in pregnancy and in active moderate-severe eye disease, which it can worsen), or thyroidectomy (rendered euthyroid first; risks recurrent laryngeal nerve injury and hypoparathyroidism). Methimazole is generally first-line outside early pregnancy.

Other thionamide adverse effects
Besides agranulocytosis (a shared class effect), PTU carries a risk of severe hepatotoxicity (and is reserved mainly for the first trimester of pregnancy and thyroid storm), while methimazole is teratogenic in the first trimester (aplasia cutis, embryopathy). Counsel patients to report fever or sore throat immediately; routine CBC monitoring is advised.
⚠ Thionamide + fever/sore throat = stop and check now
Agranulocytosis from PTU or methimazole is rare but life-threatening. Any febrile illness or sore throat warrants immediate cessation of the drug, an urgent CBC with differential, and broad-spectrum IV antibiotics for neutropenic infection. Do not 'wait and see' or simply switch thionamides.

D

Positive TPO antibodies predict progression to overt hypothyroidism and may tip the decision, but alone they are a weaker indication than a TSH >10.
B
Age over 45 is not itself an indication to treat; in older adults a higher TSH threshold is often tolerated.

Correct. This is subclinical hypothyroidism (elevated TSH, normal free T4). Reading the subclinical picture
Subclinical hypothyroidism is defined by an elevated TSH with a NORMAL free T4. Confirm with a repeat measurement (transient TSH elevations are common after illness). The strongest, guideline-supported reason to treat is a TSH persistently ≥10 µU/mL; otherwise, treat for symptoms, pregnancy or conception plans, or positive TPO antibodies with a rising TSH. In older adults a higher TSH is generally tolerated, and overtreatment risks atrial fibrillation and bone loss.

A 19-year-old man presents with 2 days of vomiting, abdominal pain, and deep, rapid breathing. He has lost weight over the past month with thirst and frequent urination. He is hypotensive and dehydrated. Labs: glucose 430 mg/dL, sodium 130 mEq/L, potassium 5.4 mEq/L, bicarbonate 8 mEq/L, chloride 96 mEq/L, pH 7.18. Serum beta-hydroxybutyrate is markedly elevated.
❓ His measured serum potassium is at the upper end of normal-to-high. Which of the following best describes his total-body potassium status and the reason?
A
Total-body potassium is elevated, reflecting true potassium overload from renal retention
B
Total-body potassium is normal, and the serum value can be taken at face value
C
Total-body potassium is elevated because insulin excess traps potassium inside cells
D
Total-body potassium is depleted because of metabolic alkalosis driving potassium into cells
E
Total-body potassium is depleted despite the high serum level, because acidosis and insulin deficiency shift potassium out of cells while osmotic diuresis wastes it
A
The high serum level is a redistribution artifact, not true overload; total-body stores are low.
B
Taking the serum value at face value risks dangerous hypokalemia once insulin is given.
C
There is insulin DEFICIENCY, not excess, in DKA.
D
DKA causes acidosis, not alkalosis; the shift direction described is wrong.
E
Correct. In diabetic ketoacidosis, insulin deficiency and acidemia drive potassium OUT of cells into the serum (masking a deficit), while the osmotic diuresis and ketoanion excretion waste large amounts of potassium in the urine. So total-body potassium is markedly DEPLETED even when the serum value looks normal or high. This is why potassium must be repleted (and confirmed >3.3) before/with starting insulin, since insulin will rapidly shift potassium back into cells.
Potassium is the safety issue
Total body potassium is depleted in DKA even when the serum level looks normal or high (acidosis and insulin deficiency shift K⁺ out of cells; osmotic diuresis wastes it). Because insulin drives potassium back into cells, give fluids first, ensure K⁺ is above ~3.3–5.3 before/with starting insulin, and replace potassium to avoid life-threatening hypokalemia. Track resolution with the anion gap and beta-hydroxybutyrate, not glucose alone, and overlap subcutaneous insulin before stopping the drip.

A
Empiric spironolactone forgoes identifying a potentially curable unilateral adenoma and is not a diagnostic confirmation.
B
Metanephrines screen for pheochromocytoma, which does not fit a hypokalemic, alkalotic, high-aldosterone/low-renin picture.

Confirm and subtype before treating
A positive aldosterone-to-renin ratio (high aldosterone, suppressed renin) is a screen, not a diagnosis. Confirm with a suppression test (e.g., saline infusion showing non-suppressible aldosterone), then obtain adrenal CT and often adrenal vein sampling to distinguish a unilateral aldosterone-producing adenoma (curable by adrenalectomy) from bilateral adrenal hyperplasia (treated medically with a mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist such as spironolactone or eplerenone). An elevated aldosterone WITH high renin instead points to secondary causes (renal artery stenosis, renal hypoperfusion).

A 35-year-old woman has 10 days of anterior neck pain radiating to the jaw, low-grade fever, and palpitations following a viral illness. The thyroid is tender. TSH is low and free T4 is mildly elevated. ESR and CRP are elevated. She is worried this is the same 'overactive thyroid' her sister has (Graves disease) and asks how they will be told apart.
❓ Which of the following findings would most reliably distinguish her condition from Graves disease?
A
The presence of palpitations and tremor
B
A suppressed TSH level
C
A diffusely enlarged thyroid
D
An elevated free T4 level
E
Low radioactive iodine uptake on scanning
A
Palpitations and tremor are shared adrenergic features.
B
A suppressed TSH occurs in both thyrotoxic states.
C
Both can produce a diffusely enlarged gland; tenderness favors thyroiditis but is less reliable than uptake.
D
An elevated free T4 occurs in both and cannot distinguish them.
E
Correct. Both subacute (de Quervain) thyroiditis and Graves cause thyrotoxic labs (low TSH, high T4) and overlapping symptoms. The discriminating test is radioiodine UPTAKE: it is LOW in subacute thyroiditis (hormone leaks from damaged follicles without new synthesis) and HIGH in Graves (active, antibody-driven synthesis). The pain and high inflammatory markers also point to thyroiditis, but uptake is the reliable separator.
The triphasic course
Subacute (de Quervain) thyroiditis classically passes through three phases: an initial THYROTOXIC phase (preformed hormone leaks from inflamed follicles, so radioiodine uptake is LOW), then a HYPOTHYROID phase as stores deplete, then RECOVERY in most patients. Because there is no ongoing hormone overproduction, antithyroid drugs are unhelpful—treat the pain/inflammation with NSAIDs (steroids if severe) and symptoms with a beta-blocker.

A 58-year-old woman is found to have a 2.5-cm thyroid nodule. She has mild palpitations. Serum TSH is 0.1 µU/mL (low) and free T4 is high-normal. Ultrasound shows a solid nodule with smooth margins. She is concerned about cancer and expects a biopsy.
❓ Which of the following is the most appropriate next step?
A
Immediate fine-needle aspiration biopsy
B
Repeat ultrasound in 12 months
C
Radioactive iodine uptake and scan
D
Start levothyroxine to suppress the nodule
E
Total thyroidectomy
A
Immediate FNA is appropriate when TSH is normal/high; here the low TSH mandates a scan first because a hot nodule does not need biopsy.
B
Deferring evaluation ignores the abnormal TSH and the need to characterize the nodule.
C
Correct. With a LOW TSH, the next step is a radioiodine scan rather than immediate biopsy. A nodule that is 'hot' (autonomously functioning) on the scan is almost never malignant and is treated for hyperthyroidism (radioiodine or surgery)—avoiding an unnecessary FNA. FNA is reserved for non-functioning ('cold') or sonographically suspicious nodules with a normal/high TSH.
D
Levothyroxine suppression is inappropriate and would worsen her subclinical hyperthyroidism.
E
Surgery is premature before functional and cytologic characterization.
Nodule workup logic
Start every thyroid nodule with a TSH and ultrasound. If TSH is LOW, obtain a radioiodine scan: a hyperfunctioning ('hot') nodule is almost never malignant and is treated for hyperthyroidism rather than biopsied. If TSH is normal or high, base the decision to do fine-needle aspiration on size and sonographic risk (solid, hypoechoic, microcalcifications, irregular margins, taller-than-wide). Cytology then guides surveillance versus surgery.

Labs: glucose 1080 mg/dL, sodium 150 mEq/L (corrected higher), potassium 3.2 mEq/L, bicarbonate 22 mEq/L, pH 7.34, with only trace serum ketones. Calculated effective serum osmolality is 340 mOsm/kg.
❓ After starting aggressive isotonic fluids, which of the following must be addressed before initiating an insulin infusion?
A
Correction of the mild acidosis with bicarbonate
B
Repletion of potassium because it is below 3.3 mEq/L
C
Administration of empiric antibiotics in the same step
D
Rapid normalization of serum sodium
E
Immediate high-dose insulin bolus to lower osmolality quickly
A
There is no significant acidosis in HHS, and bicarbonate is not indicated.
B
Correct. In the hyperosmolar hyperglycemic state (and DKA), insulin shifts potassium intracellularly. With a serum potassium already low (3.2), giving insulin first could precipitate life-threatening hypokalemia and arrhythmia. After fluids, potassium must be repleted to >3.3 mEq/L before starting the insulin infusion.
C
Treating the precipitating infection is important but is not the specific prerequisite for safely starting insulin.
D
Sodium should be corrected gradually as fluids and glucose normalize; rapid correction risks osmotic demyelination.

Find and fix the precipitant
HHS is often triggered by infection, medication nonadherence, or a new illness in an older type 2 diabetic. Management mirrors DKA—aggressive volume resuscitation, potassium repletion, then insulin—but fluid deficits are even larger and acidosis is minimal. Search for and treat the underlying precipitant, and transition to a maintenance insulin regimen once the hyperosmolar state resolves.
⚠ HHS: fluids before insulin
Like DKA, give IV isotonic fluids first and ensure potassium is repleted before insulin—insulin shifts K⁺ intracellularly and can precipitate dangerous hypokalemia. Correct osmolality gradually to avoid cerebral edema/osmotic demyelination.
USMLE Pearl: HHS/DKA insulin safety: after fluids, ensure potassium >3.3 mEq/L BEFORE starting insulin, because insulin drives K⁺ into cells. Give fluids first, replete K⁺ as needed, then insulin; correct sodium and osmolality gradually and treat the precipitant.
A 54-year-old man with type 2 diabetes is found stuporous by his wife. Paramedics measure a capillary glucose of 18 mg/dL. He is not arousable enough to swallow safely, and repeated attempts to establish intravenous access in the field fail. His wife reports he recently had a medication change but cannot recall the name. This is the first of two linked questions about the same patient.
❓ Which of the following is the most appropriate immediate treatment given the lack of IV access?
A
Subcutaneous rapid-acting insulin
B
Intramuscular octreotide
C
Wait and re-attempt IV access before any treatment
D
Oral glucose gel applied to the buccal mucosa
E
Intramuscular glucagon
A
Insulin would deepen the hypoglycemia and is dangerous.
B
Octreotide is an adjunct for recurrent sulfonylurea-induced hypoglycemia, not the field rescue for an obtunded patient—glucagon is.
C
Delaying treatment to keep attempting IV access risks neuroglycopenic injury.
D
Buccal/oral glucose risks aspiration in someone who cannot swallow safely.
E
Correct. With profound hypoglycemia, an unprotected airway, and no IV access, intramuscular (or intranasal) glucagon is the immediate treatment to mobilize hepatic glucose. Oral/buccal glucose is unsafe in a stuporous patient (aspiration), and treatment should not be delayed.
Recognize and reverse hypoglycemia
Hypoglycemia produces autonomic symptoms (sweating, tremor, palpitations, hunger, anxiety) and neuroglycopenic symptoms (confusion, visual change, seizures, coma). Recurrent episodes blunt awareness and raise future risk. Awake patients get oral carbohydrate; obtunded patients get IV dextrose (with access) or IM/intranasal glucagon (without access). Patients with type 1 diabetes and CKD are especially prone—reduce insulin and lower the dose before exercise.
⚠ Unconscious + no IV access = IM/intranasal glucagon
Never put oral glucose in the mouth of an obtunded patient (aspiration). If there is no IV access, give intramuscular or intranasal glucagon immediately, then obtain access for IV dextrose once feasible.
USMLE Pearl: Severe hypoglycemia, can't protect airway, no IV access → IM/intranasal glucagon now. Awake or IV access → oral carbs or IV dextrose. Never give oral glucose to an obtunded patient. Glucagon needs hepatic glycogen, so its effect can be brief—follow with glucose.

Why C-peptide and the drug screen matter later
When the cause of hypoglycemia is unclear, the insulin/C-peptide/sulfonylurea-screen pattern distinguishes mechanisms: endogenous hyperinsulinism (insulinoma) shows high insulin + high C-peptide + negative screen; exogenous insulin shows high insulin + LOW C-peptide; and sulfonylurea shows high insulin + high C-peptide + POSITIVE screen. In a known diabetic on a sulfonylurea, the drug itself is the obvious culprit—treat first.
⚠ Sulfonylurea hypoglycemia recurs — add octreotide
Because sulfonylureas keep stimulating endogenous insulin, hypoglycemia often returns despite dextrose. Octreotide suppresses insulin release and is the adjunct for recurrent/refractory sulfonylurea-induced hypoglycemia. Long-acting agents and renal impairment prolong the danger—admit and monitor.
USMLE Pearl: Sulfonylurea hypoglycemia recurs because the drug keeps stimulating insulin secretion—give dextrose PLUS octreotide to suppress insulin. Renal impairment and long-acting agents prolong risk; admit and monitor rather than discharging after one correction.

Confirming and localizing an insulinoma
A supervised 72-hour fast that reproduces hypoglycemia with inappropriately elevated insulin, C-peptide, and proinsulin—and a negative sulfonylurea screen—establishes endogenous hyperinsulinism. The next step is pancreatic localization (CT/MRI, endoscopic ultrasound). Most insulinomas are small, solitary, benign beta-cell tumors cured by resection; consider MEN1 if there are other endocrine tumors.

Beyond glycemic control, choose agents for their organ-protective effects: an SGLT2 inhibitor for heart failure or chronic kidney disease, and an SGLT2 inhibitor or GLP-1 receptor agonist for established atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease. A 56-year-old woman has a serum calcium of 11.4 mg/dL on routine labs, confirmed on repeat with a normal albumin. She has mild fatigue and a remote kidney stone. Phosphate is low-normal, and intact PTH is 78 pg/mL (reference 15–65). 24-hour urine calcium is elevated. Renal function is normal, and she takes no thiazide or lithium.
❓ Which feature most strongly distinguishes her diagnosis from familial hypocalciuric hypercalcemia (FHH)?
A
The mildly elevated serum calcium
B
The low-normal serum phosphate
C
The elevated 24-hour urine calcium
D
The presence of fatigue
E
The inappropriately non-suppressed PTH
A
Mild hypercalcemia occurs in both conditions and does not distinguish them.
B
Low-normal phosphate is consistent with PTH excess but is not the key discriminator from FHH.
C
Correct. Both primary hyperparathyroidism and FHH show hypercalcemia with an inappropriately normal/high PTH, so PTH does NOT separate them. Calcium follows PTH
Work up hypercalcemia by measuring PTH after confirming the value (with albumin/ionized calcium). A 64-year-old man with a 40-pack-year smoking history and recent weight loss is admitted with confusion and a calcium of 14.2 mg/dL. Phosphate is low. Intact PTH is suppressed. He has no bone pain, and a skeletal survey shows no lytic lesions. A chest mass is noted on imaging.
❓ Which of the following laboratory findings would best confirm the most likely mechanism of his hypercalcemia?
A
Elevated intact PTH
B
Elevated serum protein gap with monoclonal spike
C
Elevated 25-hydroxyvitamin D
D
Elevated PTH-related peptide (PTHrP)
E
Elevated 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D
A
An elevated PTH would indicate primary hyperparathyroidism, but his PTH is suppressed.
B
A monoclonal spike suggests myeloma (which causes hypercalcemia via osteolysis), but he has no lytic lesions and has a lung mass—PTHrP is the better fit.
C
Elevated 25-hydroxyvitamin D indicates vitamin D intake/intoxication, not a paraneoplastic process.
D
Correct. Hypercalcemia with a SUPPRESSED PTH, low phosphate, no lytic lesions, and a lung mass in a heavy smoker points to humoral hypercalcemia of malignancy from PTHrP (classically squamous cell lung carcinoma). E
Elevated 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D characterizes granulomatous disease or lymphoma-mediated hypercalcemia, not the PTHrP mechanism suggested by the lung mass.
Mechanisms of tumor hypercalcemia
Malignancy raises calcium by several routes: humoral hypercalcemia via PTHrP (classically squamous cell lung carcinoma and renal cell carcinoma), osteolytic bone metastases, and tumor activation of 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D (lymphoma). In all of these, native PTH is SUPPRESSED—distinguishing them from primary hyperparathyroidism.
⚠ Hypercalcemia of malignancy is an emergency
Severe hypercalcemia causes lethargy, dehydration, and can progress to coma. Treat with aggressive IV isotonic saline FIRST (volume repletion), then a bisphosphonate ± calcitonin. A SUPPRESSED PTH with elevated PTHrP localizes the cause to a tumor.
USMLE Pearl: Hypercalcemia + suppressed PTH + low phosphate + lung mass → humoral hypercalcemia of malignancy (PTHrP, classically squamous cell lung cancer). Confirm with elevated PTHrP. Other tumor mechanisms: 1,25-vitamin D (lymphoma/granuloma) and osteolysis (myeloma, mets). Treat: IV saline then bisphosphonate.
A 47-year-old man with rheumatoid arthritis on long-term prednisone (recently self-tapered) undergoes urgent incarcerated-hernia repair. On postoperative day 1 he becomes confused and hypotensive (80/46 mm Hg). He receives 3 liters of isotonic fluid and a fluid challenge without improvement. He is afebrile; cultures are pending. Sodium is 131 mEq/L, potassium 4.6 mEq/L, and glucose is 62 mg/dL. This is the first of two linked questions about the same patient.
❓ Which of the following best explains his fluid-refractory hypotension?
A
Inadequate cortisol response from a suppressed hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis
B
Cardiogenic shock from perioperative myocardial infarction
C
Distributive shock from intra-abdominal sepsis
D
Hypovolemia from inadequate fluid resuscitation
E
Anaphylaxis to a perioperative antibiotic
A
Correct. Chronic glucocorticoids suppress the HPA axis and atrophy the adrenal cortex; an abrupt taper plus the stress of surgery produces an inadequate cortisol response—acute (secondary) adrenal insufficiency. The clues are fluid-refractory hypotension, confusion, mild hyponatremia, and hypoglycemia in a steroid-dependent patient. (Potassium is often normal in SECONDARY insufficiency because aldosterone is preserved.)
B
There is no evidence of pump failure or ischemia to indicate cardiogenic shock.
C
Sepsis is possible but he is afebrile with pending cultures, and the steroid history with fluid-refractory shock and hypoglycemia points to adrenal insufficiency as the best explanation.
D
He has received substantial fluids without response, arguing against simple hypovolemia.
E
Anaphylaxis presents intraoperatively with urticaria/bronchospasm, not POD1 fluid-refractory shock with hypoglycemia.
Why chronic steroids set up the crisis
Chronic exogenous glucocorticoids suppress the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis and atrophy the adrenal cortex. A major stressor (surgery, infection) then demands a cortisol surge the suppressed axis cannot produce, causing acute adrenal insufficiency. Prevention is stress-dose steroid coverage around surgery and illness; meningococcemia (Waterhouse-Friderichsen) is another classic precipitant of acute adrenal failure.
⚠ Adrenal crisis: treat before you test
In a steroid-dependent patient who decompensates under stress with fluid-refractory hypotension and confusion, give IV hydrocortisone immediately along with fluids and glucose/electrolyte correction—do not delay for confirmatory cortisol/ACTH results.
USMLE Pearl: Steroid-dependent patient + surgical stress + abrupt taper → acute adrenal insufficiency: fluid-refractory hypotension, confusion, hyponatremia, hypoglycemia. Secondary (steroid-induced) insufficiency usually has NORMAL potassium (aldosterone preserved), unlike primary Addison disease.

Acute adrenal crisis is treated with IV hydrocortisone (which at stress doses also covers mineralocorticoid needs), aggressive fluids, and correction of hypoglycemia and electrolytes. Chronic primary adrenal insufficiency requires BOTH a glucocorticoid and a mineralocorticoid (fludrocortisone); steroid-dependent patients need increased ('stress-dose') glucocorticoid around surgery, trauma, or significant illness to prevent crisis.

A 45-year-old woman has paroxysms of pounding headache, palpitations, and diaphoresis with blood pressures up to 210/120 mm Hg between normal readings. Plasma free metanephrines are markedly elevated and abdominal imaging shows a 5-cm adrenal mass. Surgery is planned. A colleague suggests starting metoprolol today to control her tachycardia.
❓ Why is starting a beta-blocker first the wrong initial step in her preoperative preparation?
A
Beta-blockers blunt the metanephrine assay and obscure monitoring
B
Beta-blockers cause reflex bradycardia that lowers tumor perfusion
C
Unopposed alpha-adrenergic vasoconstriction can precipitate a hypertensive crisis
D
Beta-blockers cause the tumor to enlarge before surgery
E
Beta-blockers worsen catecholamine synthesis by the tumor
A
Beta-blockers do not interfere with the metanephrine assay.
B
The danger is unopposed alpha vasoconstriction/hypertensive crisis, not reduced tumor perfusion.
C
Correct. In pheochromocytoma, catecholamines act on both alpha (vasoconstriction) and beta (vasodilation/heart rate) receptors. Diagnosis and the MEN link
Diagnose pheochromocytoma with plasma free metanephrines or 24-hour urine metanephrines/catecholamines, then localize with abdominal imaging. Episodic headache, palpitations, and sweating with hypertension are the classic triad. ⚠ Pheochromocytoma: alpha-block BEFORE beta-block
Always establish alpha-blockade (e.g., phenoxybenzamine) and volume-expand before adding a beta-blocker. Beta-blockade first leaves alpha-mediated vasoconstriction unopposed and can trigger a hypertensive crisis. Resect only after adequate medical preparation.

Prolactinoma management
A symptomatic prolactinoma is treated medically first with a dopamine agonist (cabergoline preferred over bromocriptine), which lowers prolactin, restores fertility/gonadal function, and shrinks the tumor—even macroadenomas. A 33-year-old woman is seen 7 weeks after a delivery complicated by severe hemorrhage and hypotension requiring transfusion. She reports fatigue, anorexia, lightheadedness on standing, cold intolerance, and an inability to breastfeed. ❓ Which laboratory feature most specifically points to a pituitary rather than a primary end-organ cause of her hypothyroidism?
A
A low free T4 with a non-elevated (inappropriately normal) TSH
B

C
The low serum sodium
D
The low morning cortisol
E
The inability to breastfeed
A
Correct. In PRIMARY hypothyroidism the failing thyroid drives TSH UP. Here free T4 is low but TSH is NOT elevated (inappropriately normal), indicating the pituitary is not responding—i.e., CENTRAL hypothyroidism. Combined with lactation failure and secondary adrenal insufficiency after postpartum hemorrhage, this indicates Sheehan syndrome (pituitary infarction). The low-T4/low-or-normal-TSH mismatch is the specific pituitary clue.
B
Cold intolerance and fatigue are nonspecific hypothyroid symptoms.
C
Low sodium can occur from cortisol deficiency or hypothyroidism but is nonspecific to a pituitary cause.
D
Low cortisol indicates adrenal insufficiency but, by itself, does not localize to the pituitary (could be primary adrenal).
E
Lactation failure suggests low prolactin and supports Sheehan, but the most SPECIFIC localizing lab for the hypothyroidism axis is the low T4 with non-elevated TSH.
Sheehan and the broader pituitary picture
Sheehan syndrome is postpartum pituitary infarction after major obstetric hemorrhage, producing panhypopituitarism: failure to lactate (low prolactin), central hypothyroidism (low T4 with low/normal TSH), and secondary adrenal insufficiency (low ACTH/cortisol), sometimes with diabetes insipidus. Replace the deficient hormones—glucocorticoid first if adrenal insufficiency is suspected, then levothyroxine—and remember that pituitary mass effects (e.g., a macroprolactinoma or other adenoma) can also cause multi-axis hypopituitarism.
